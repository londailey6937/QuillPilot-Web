üìè Spacing Analysis Reference

What is Spacing Analysis?

Spacing analysis evaluates how concepts are distributed across your chapter to support spaced repetition - a proven learning technique where information is revisited at optimal intervals.

Paragraph Spacing Indicators

When you upload a document, spacing indicators appear between paragraphs showing word counts and density:

Indicator Color Word Count Range Meaning Action
üü¢ Green (Good) 60-160 words Optimal paragraph length for learning Keep this density
üü° Amber (Compact) <60 words Too brief, may lack detail Consider expanding
üî¥ Orange (Extended) >160 words Too long, cognitive overload risk Split into smaller chunks

How Spacing Scores Are Calculated

Score Range: 0-100

  • 80-100 (High): Strong spaced repetition with concepts revisited 3-5 times at good intervals
  • 50-79 (Medium): Moderate spacing, some concepts repeated but intervals could be better
  • 0-49 (Low): Poor spacing, concepts either mentioned once or clustered together

Optimal Spacing Patterns

‚úÖ Good Example:

Introduction ‚Üí Concept A introduced
Section 2 ‚Üí Concept A mentioned in context (80 words later)
Section 4 ‚Üí Concept A applied in example (200 words later)
Summary ‚Üí Concept A reviewed (300 words later)

‚ùå Poor Example:

Section 1 ‚Üí Concept A introduced
Section 1 ‚Üí Concept A mentioned 3 times in rapid succession
[Never mentioned again in rest of chapter]

Spacing Targets Explained

The dashed lines between paragraphs mark "spacing targets" - ideal locations where concepts should be revisited. The system analyzes:

  • Gap size between concept mentions
  • Distribution across chapter sections
  • Density of paragraphs (words per paragraph)
  • Cognitive load based on paragraph length

üé® Dual Coding Analysis Reference

What is Dual Coding?

Dual coding combines verbal (text) and visual (images, diagrams) representations to enhance learning and memory retention.

Visual Suggestion Types

The analyzer detects passages that would benefit from visuals and suggests specific formats:

Visual Type When Suggested Example Text Patterns
üìä Diagram Spatial relationships, structures "consists of three layers", "connected to"
üß≠ Flowchart Processes, sequences, steps "first step", "then proceed", "finally"
üìà Graph Data, trends, comparisons "increased by 40%", "correlation between"
üé® Illustration Physical descriptions "shaped like", "appears as", "resembles"
üß† Concept Map Abstract relationships "relates to", "category of", "consists of"

Priority Levels

Each suggestion has a priority indicating urgency:

Priority Badge Color Meaning When Assigned
High Priority üî¥ Red Critical - Complex content suffers without visual Dense technical passages, multi-step processes
Medium Priority üü° Orange Beneficial - Would improve comprehension Comparisons, moderate complexity
Low Priority ‚ö™ Gray Optional - Minor enhancement Simple descriptions, supplementary details

How Dual Coding Scores Are Calculated

Score Range: 0-100

  • 80-100 (High): Excellent visual support with diagrams/images for complex concepts
  • 50-79 (Medium): Some visual elements present but opportunities missed
  • 0-49 (Low): Text-heavy with little to no visual representation

Dual Coding Callouts Structure

When you see a yellow callout box in Writer Mode or exports, it contains:

  1. Icon + Title: Visual type (e.g., "üìä Diagram - High Priority")
  2. Reason: Why this visual is needed (e.g., "Complex spatial relationships detected")
  3. Context: Quote from your text triggering the suggestion
  4. Suggested Action: Specific recommendation (e.g., "Create a diagram showing the three-layer architecture")

üìö Domain Libraries Explained

What Are Domain Libraries?

Domain libraries are pre-built concept dictionaries tailored to specific subject areas. They help the analyzer:

  • Recognize subject-specific terminology
  • Extract domain concepts more accurately
  • Evaluate content relevance to the field

Built-in Domains

Domain Concept Count Best For
Chemistry 150+ General chemistry, organic, reactions, lab techniques
Algebra & Trig 120+ Mathematical concepts, equations, functions
Computing 180+ Programming, data structures, algorithms, systems
Finance 90+ Financial concepts, markets, accounting, economics
React 85+ React.js development, hooks, components, state management

Auto-Detection Feature

When you upload a document, Chapter Checker analyzes your content and suggests the most likely domain based on:

  • Keyword analysis - Detecting domain-specific terminology
  • Concept frequency - How often domain concepts appear
  • Confidence scoring - Accuracy of the detection

üí° Detected Domain

After uploading, you'll see a "Detected Domain" section showing the auto-detected subject area. You can:

  • ‚úÖ Accept the detected domain if it looks correct
  • üîÑ Click "Change Domain" to select a different one
  • üéØ Use the dropdown to choose from all available domains

Tip: Always verify the detected domain matches your chapter's primary subject for best results!

How Domains Affect Analysis

‚úÖ With Correct Domain:

Text: "The activation energy determines reaction rate"
Recognized: ‚úÖ "activation energy", "reaction rate" (both core chemistry concepts)
Score Impact: Higher concept density, better evaluation

‚ùå Without Domain (or wrong domain):

Text: "The activation energy determines reaction rate"
Recognized: ‚ùå Generic terms only
Score Impact: Lower scores, missed learning opportunities

üé® Custom Domain Creation

‚ö†Ô∏è Professional Tier Feature

Custom domain creation requires a Professional tier subscription. Premium users can use all built-in domains.

When to Create Custom Domains

Create custom domains when working with:

  • Specialized subjects - Marine biology, ancient history, medical terminology
  • Corporate training - Industry-specific jargon and processes
  • Academic research - Novel terminology not in standard dictionaries
  • K-12 curricula - Grade-appropriate concept vocabulary
  • Emerging fields - New technologies or methodologies

Step-by-Step: Creating Your Domain

1. Access Custom Domains

  1. Upload your chapter document first
  2. Click the ‚ò∞ Navigation menu button (top-left)
  3. Select "Custom Domains" from the menu
  4. Click "+ Create New Domain"

2. Name Your Domain

Domain Name Tips:

  • ‚úÖ Use descriptive names: "Marine Biology 101", "Corporate Finance 2025"
  • ‚úÖ Include course/book identifiers: "Intro Chemistry Spring 2025"
  • ‚úÖ Keep it under 50 characters for readability
  • ‚ùå Avoid generic names like "My Domain" or "Test"

3. Add Concepts

You have two methods for adding concepts:

üìù Manual Entry

Best for: Small domains (10-50 concepts)

Steps:

  1. Click "+ Add Concept"
  2. Enter the concept name (e.g., "photosynthesis")
  3. Select category: Core, Supporting, or Detail
  4. Optionally add a definition
  5. Click "Save Concept"
  6. Repeat for each concept

üìã Bulk Import

Best for: Large domains (50+ concepts)

Steps:

  1. Click "Bulk Import"
  2. Prepare a list (one concept per line)
  3. Paste into the text area
  4. Select default category for all
  5. Click "Import Concepts"
  6. Review and edit individually if needed

Concept Categories Explained

Category Purpose How Many? Examples
Core Foundation ideas students MUST understand 10-15 per chapter "photosynthesis", "cell membrane", "DNA replication"
Supporting Important but not central to main concepts 20-30 per chapter "chloroplast", "phospholipid bilayer", "base pairs"
Detail Specific terms, technical vocabulary 40-60 per chapter "thylakoid", "glycerol molecule", "adenine"

Adding Concept Definitions (Optional)

Definitions help you remember what each concept means, but they're not required for analysis:

‚úÖ Good Definition:

Concept: photosynthesis

Definition: "Process by which plants convert light energy into chemical energy (glucose)"

Why it's good: Concise, accurate, includes key mechanism

‚ö†Ô∏è Unnecessary Definition:

Concept: photosynthesis

Definition: "A word that appears in Chapter 5"

Why skip it: Not helpful for understanding or analysis

Best Practices

üìè Domain Size Guidelines

  • Small domain: 20-50 concepts for focused chapters
  • Medium domain: 50-100 concepts for standard textbook chapters
  • Large domain: 100-200 concepts for comprehensive courses
  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Too large: Over 300 concepts may slow analysis

‚úÖ Quality Tips

  • Consistent terminology - Match your textbook's exact wording
  • Include synonyms - Add variations if concepts have multiple names
  • Lowercase preferred - Use "cell membrane" not "Cell Membrane"
  • Avoid abbreviations - Add both "DNA" and "deoxyribonucleic acid"
  • Test first - Analyze a sample chapter before finalizing
  • Iterate - Add missed terms as you discover them

Using Your Custom Domain

  1. Save your custom domain (it's stored in your browser)
  2. Go back to the main upload screen
  3. Upload your chapter document
  4. In the "Detected Domain" section, click "Change Domain"
  5. Select your custom domain from the dropdown
  6. Click "Analyze with [Your Domain]"

‚ö†Ô∏è How Custom Domains Are Stored

Custom domains use browser localStorage - a client-side storage mechanism:

  • ‚úÖ Stored locally - All data stays on YOUR computer, never uploaded to servers
  • ‚úÖ Persistent - Domains remain even after closing the browser or restarting your computer
  • ‚úÖ Fast access - No network calls, instant loading
  • ‚úÖ Privacy - Your custom concepts are 100% private
  • ‚ùå Per-browser - Chrome domains won't appear in Firefox (they're separate)
  • ‚ùå Per-device - Laptop domains won't sync to your tablet or phone
  • ‚ùå Cleared with browser data - Using "Clear browsing data" will delete them

üí° Backup Strategy:

  • Copy your concept list to a text file before clearing browser data
  • Keep a spreadsheet of important domains for easy re-import
  • Take screenshots of complex domain configurations

üîß Technical Details:

Storage Key: "tomeiq_custom_domains"
Format: JSON array of domain objects
Max Size: ~5-10MB (browser dependent)
Location: Browser's IndexedDB/localStorage
